# Stewardship - Implementation
### Groupthink - Version 0.6

---

## What This Is

This document offers starting points for how a group can steward shared resources within the Groupthink framework.

---

## Identifying the Commons

When a group begins managing a shared resource, it should identify:

1. **What is the resource?** Name it specifically. A community garden, a shared server, a tool library, a stretch of river.
2. **What are its boundaries?** Physical or functional boundaries that define where this commons begins and ends.
3. **Who depends on it?** The stakeholders - including people outside the group.
4. **What can it sustain?** The best current understanding of the resource's capacity, based on observation and available knowledge.
5. **Who maintains it?** The stewardship roles - who is responsible for what, and how they are accountable.

This identification is recorded and revisited as understanding changes.

---

## The Invocation Process

When a stakeholder invokes the commons:

1. The invocation must cite specific, observable evidence that a decision exceeds what the resource can sustain.
2. The decision pauses until the evidence is examined.
3. The group examines the evidence. If expertise is needed and not available within the group, it is sought.
4. The group records its finding: does the evidence support the invocation?
5. If yes, the decision is modified or withdrawn.
6. If no, the group records why and proceeds.
7. The invocation and the full response are part of the permanent record.

An invocation applies only to decisions that touch shared resources. The evidence requirement exists to ensure good-faith use.

---

## Stewardship Roles

For built commons - shared infrastructure, tools, systems - the group defines stewardship roles:

1. A steward has delegated authority to maintain, repair, and operate the commons.
2. Stewardship is a responsibility. Stewards carry their scope of care and voice decisions only within it.
3. Stewards are accountable to the group and can be recalled through the group's decision process.
4. Stewardship should rotate where practical, to prevent the role from becoming a power position.

---

## Contested Use of Shared Resources

When use of a shared resource is contested:

1. The character of use matters. Personal use is unconditionally protected. Collective investment creates legitimate stakes.
2. The contest must be resolved through the group's decision process.
3. Sustainability limits override all claims. No resolution can exceed what the commons can sustain.
4. The resolution and its reasoning are recorded.

---

## Coordination Between Commons

When one commons' decisions affect another:

1. The affected stakeholders have standing to participate in the decision.
2. Coordination follows the same principles as coordination between groups - voluntary, bounded, transparent.
3. If the two commons are governed by different groups, inter-group coordination applies. *(See Documents/Federation/.)*

---

## Commons-Based Federations

When multiple groups depend on the same physical resource, the resource itself creates the basis for coordination. A commons-based federation exists because the resource exists — its scope is defined by the resource's physical boundaries, not by group agreement.

### How they form

1. Any stakeholder group can propose a commons-based federation by identifying the resource, its physical boundaries, and the groups that depend on it.
2. All identified stakeholder groups are invited. Participation is voluntary — no group is compelled to join.
3. The federation's scope is defined by the resource. It cannot expand beyond the resource's boundaries without becoming a voluntary federation.

### Voice in a commons-based federation

Unlike voluntary federations where each group has one voice regardless of size, voice in a commons-based federation is proportional to **stake** — how much a group depends on and affects the resource.

1. Each participating group declares its stake: what it draws from the resource, what it contributes to the resource, and how many people depend on its use.
2. Stake declarations are recorded and subject to challenge by other stakeholders.
3. Voice is weighted by stake. A group that draws 60% of a shared water supply has a larger voice in decisions about that supply than a group that draws 2%.
4. Stake is reassessed periodically as conditions change.

### Sustainability findings

The primary function of a commons-based federation is to determine what the resource can sustain — and to publish that finding.

1. Sustainability findings are based on observation, measurement, and the best available knowledge.
2. Findings are published to all stakeholder groups, including those that chose not to participate.
3. A sustainability finding is not a governance decision — it is a description of physical reality. A group may dispute the finding's accuracy, but it cannot dispute the principle that the resource has limits.
4. When a group's use exceeds what a sustainability finding says the resource can sustain, any stakeholder may invoke the commons. *(See Documents/Stewardship/1.Rights.md §2.)*

### Nesting

Commons-based federations nest following the resource's physical structure. A creek's commons-based federation nests within the river's, which nests within the watershed's. At each layer, scope narrows to the concerns that genuinely span all members. Higher layers address broader sustainability questions; lower layers address local use and maintenance.
