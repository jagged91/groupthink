# Provision - Implementation
### Groupthink - Version 0.6

---

## What This Is

This document offers starting points for groups that provide essential needs to their members.

---

## Recognising Provision

A group should explicitly acknowledge when it provides essentials. This is a structural condition that triggers additional responsibilities. The group records:

1. What essentials does the group provide? (Shelter, food, water, energy, healthcare, livelihood, safety.)
2. Which members depend on the group for these essentials?
3. What alternatives exist? Where are they? How accessible are they?
4. What happens to a member's access to essentials if they leave?

This assessment is revisited regularly. Dependency can develop gradually without anyone noticing.

---

## Ensuring Genuine Exit

When a group provides essentials, it should establish:

1. **Transition support.** A defined process for departing members that maintains access to essentials for a transition period (to be defined by the group, but sufficient to find alternatives).
2. **Portable resources.** Where possible, resources that a member has contributed to or built should be portable - transferable to a new context.
3. **Alternative mapping.** The group maintains and shares a current map of alternative providers and systems, so that no member is ever unaware of their options. Groups providing essentials should participate in **inter-group alternative mapping** — sharing their alternative maps with other providing groups so that the network collectively maintains awareness of where essentials are available. This cooperation strengthens genuine exit across the entire system, not just within a single group.
4. **No conditional essentials.** The group explicitly separates provision of essentials from participation requirements. A member in dispute, in structured separation, or choosing to leave continues to receive essentials during transition.

---

## Structural Health Checks

Any member can raise a structural concern - the claim that the group has become a trap. The group should also conduct periodic self-assessments:

1. Can every member leave tomorrow and survive?
2. Does every member know their alternatives?
3. Has the group become the sole source of any essential for any member?
4. If yes, what is the group doing about it?

These assessments are recorded.

---

## Building Toward Self-Sufficiency

Provision is a function. Over time, a providing group should:

1. Help members develop independent capacity where possible.
2. Connect members to multiple sources of essentials, reducing single-source dependency.
3. Support the creation of new groups and systems that provide alternatives.
4. Share knowledge about provision openly, so that other groups can replicate what works.

The direction is toward a world where provision is distributed widely enough that no single group holds structural power over anyone's survival.
