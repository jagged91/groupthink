# Accountability - Implementation
### Groupthink - Version 0.6

---

## What This Is

This document offers starting points for how a group can exercise its accountability function — the capacity to respond to harm, pursue repair, and maintain the integrity of the framework's commitments. Like defense, this function carries real danger. Every suggestion below is designed to constrain it.

---

## Triggering Accountability

1. Any member or stakeholder can raise a harm claim — the assertion that a person or group has caused identifiable harm.
2. The claim must describe the harm, identify who was affected, and present the evidence.
3. The group examines the claim. The person or group named in the claim is notified immediately and given full opportunity to respond before any action is taken.
4. If the claim involves a floor violation, it cannot be blocked or dismissed by the party named. The group must examine it.

---

## Structural Examination

Before determining consequences, the group examines the conditions that produced the harm:

1. What pressures, scarcities, or structural failures contributed? Was the person hungry, excluded, desperate, or trapped?
2. Did the group's own rules or structures create conditions that made the harm more likely?
3. Could the harm have been prevented by structural changes that were within the group's power?
4. The examination is recorded in full. Its findings inform every subsequent step.

The structural examination does not replace individual responsibility. A person who caused harm is still responsible for repair. But a group that skips the structural examination is treating symptoms while the disease continues.

---

## The Repair Process

1. The harmed party describes what repair would mean to them. Their account is central.
2. The person or group that caused the harm is invited to propose their own path to repair. Voluntary repair is always preferred.
3. If both parties can agree on a repair path, the agreement is recorded and the group monitors its completion.
4. If voluntary repair is not forthcoming, the group deliberates on a repair path using its normal decision process.
5. The repair path must be specific, achievable, and proportional to the harm.
6. Structural changes — to the group's rules, resource allocation, or practices — are part of repair when the examination identifies structural causes.

---

## Structured Separation

When a person or group has caused harm and does not make repair, the community may impose structured separation:

1. **Specificity.** The separation names exactly what is withdrawn — access to specific shared resources, participation in specific decisions, membership in specific circles — and why.
2. **Proportionality.** The scope of separation is proportional to the harm. Minor harm warrants narrow separation. A floor violation may warrant broad separation, including network-standing consequences.
3. **Duration.** Every separation has a defined duration. At expiry, the community must actively decide to renew.
4. **Restoration path.** Every separation includes explicit conditions for early restoration — what the separated party must do to repair the harm and restore the relationship.
5. **Preserved rights.** The separated party retains: all individual Core rights, access to essentials (when the group provides them), membership in all other groups, the right to form new groups, and the right to challenge the finding.
6. **Independent review.** Before separation takes effect, the finding and the proposed separation are reviewed by people with no stake in the outcome.

---

## Network-Standing Consequences

When a group crosses a floor and an independent review confirms the violation, the consequences extend beyond the group:

1. **Publication.** The finding — the harm, the evidence, the review process, and the restoration path — is published to all groups in the violating group's federations and compacts.
2. **Standing suspension.** The violating group's Groupthink standing is suspended. This means: other groups are informed that the group is not currently honouring the commitments that define the framework. Other groups make their own decisions about how to respond.
3. **Coordinated separation.** Federations and compacts may choose to suspend the violating group's participation — through their own decision processes, subject to their own safeguards.
4. **Restoration.** The finding defines what repair looks like. When the violating group makes repair, it requests a follow-up independent review. If the review confirms restoration, standing is restored and the restoration is published with the same visibility as the original finding.
5. **Expiry.** Standing suspension has a defined duration. At expiry, the suspension must be actively renewed or it lapses. The burden is on the network to justify continued suspension.

Standing suspension is not exile. The group continues to exist. Its members retain all individual rights in every other group. The group can form new relationships with any group willing to associate with it. The suspension is a signal, not a cage.

---

## The Relationship Between Accountability and Defense

The boundary is clear:

- **Defense** prevents irreversible harm that is occurring or imminent. It may use force. It activates and deactivates around a specific threat.
- **Accountability** responds to harm that has occurred. It uses separation, not force. It pursues repair, not prevention.

When defense has been exercised — force was used to prevent irreversible harm — accountability follows. The defensive action is reviewed (Documents/Defense/2.Implementation.md § Review). If the harm was caused by a person or group, accountability examines the structural causes and pursues repair. If the defensive action itself was disproportionate, accountability applies to the defenders as well.

---

## The Danger

Accountability institutions are among the most dangerous structures humans have built. Courts, police, prisons — systems created to address harm have become the largest sources of harm in many societies. Every safeguard in this document exists because of that history.

A group that exercises accountability should treat it as a wound — necessary sometimes, and always to be healed as quickly as possible. The goal is always to return to a state where voluntary repair, reputation, and exit are sufficient.

The specific dangers to watch for:

1. **Accountability as control.** Using the process to suppress dissent, punish inconvenient people, or enforce conformity.
2. **Permanent separation.** Allowing separations to continue indefinitely without active reauthorization.
3. **Disproportionate response.** Network-standing consequences for harms that do not involve floor violations.
4. **Structural examination as excuse.** Using structural causes to avoid holding anyone responsible for anything.
5. **The accountability class.** Allowing a subset of members to become specialists in accountability, accumulating power through the role.

Rotation, transparency, independent review, expiry, and the right to challenge — these exist to resist these dangers. They will not always succeed. Vigilance is permanent.
