# Accountability - Values
### Groupthink - Version 0.6

---

## When This Applies

A group **accounts** when it holds persons or groups accountable for harm — when someone has caused damage and the question is what happens next. This is the function that addresses the space between soft mechanisms (reputation, exit, dispute resolution) and defense (preventing irreversible harm in progress).

Most harm in human systems has structural causes. A person who acts badly is usually responding to conditions — scarcity, exclusion, desperation, broken systems. Accountability begins by examining these conditions. It does not end there.

These values build on the Core (Documents/Core/0.Values.md) and extend it.

---

## The Principle

The Groupthink framework relies on voluntary association, reputation, and exit as its primary mechanisms for resolving disputes. These mechanisms work well for most disagreements. They reach their limit when real harm has occurred and the person or group responsible does not voluntarily make repair.

Defense addresses harm that is irreversible and in progress. Accountability addresses harm that has occurred — recoverable or not — and the question of what repair looks like. Defense prevents. Accountability responds.

Every system of accountability in history has faced the same danger: the institutions built to address harm become instruments of harm themselves. Courts become tools of the powerful. Prisons become warehouses. Justice becomes vengeance wearing a procedural mask. This function exists with that history in full view.

---

## The Values

### Harm is structural before it is personal

When harm occurs, the first question is not "who do we blame?" but "what conditions produced this?" A person who steals food is usually hungry. A group that pollutes a river usually lacks alternatives. Examining the structure that produced the harm is not an excuse — it is a prerequisite for preventing the harm from recurring.

Personal responsibility remains. A person who causes harm is responsible for repair. But a system that only addresses individual behaviour while leaving the structure intact will produce the same harm again, carried out by someone else.

### Repair is the purpose

The goal of accountability is to restore what was damaged — the person harmed, the relationship broken, the resource degraded, and the conditions that produced the harm. Every element of accountability should serve repair. Any element that does not serve repair should be questioned.

Punishment that restores nothing is vengeance. The framework does not prohibit consequences — it requires that consequences serve a restorative purpose.

### Accountability is a wound

Exercising accountability over another person or group is inherently dangerous. It creates power asymmetry. It risks abuse. It should be entered reluctantly, conducted transparently, and ended as quickly as possible. The same philosophy that governs defense governs accountability: necessary sometimes, always to be healed.

### Separation is the primary tool

When a person or group causes harm and does not voluntarily make repair, the framework's primary response is structured separation — the withdrawal of specific relationships, access, and cooperation. Separation is not exile. The person retains all individual rights. The group retains the right to form new relationships. Separation is the community saying: "We will not cooperate with you while this harm stands unrepaired."

Separation must be specific (naming what is withdrawn and why), bounded (with a defined duration and review), and reversible (with a clear path to restoration).

### Restoration is always defined

Every accountability action includes a defined path back. What does the person or group need to do to restore the relationship? What does repair look like? If restoration is impossible to define, the accountability action should be questioned — it may be serving punishment rather than repair.

### The record is the consequence

In a system built on transparency and reputation, the most powerful consequence is the truth. What happened is permanently recorded, independently reviewed, and visible to all. A group that crossed a floor carries that finding in its record forever. The record does not punish — it informs. Other groups and persons make their own decisions based on what the record shows.

### Floor violations carry network standing

The floors are universal commitments. Every Groupthink group honours them by calling itself Groupthink. When a group crosses a floor and an independent review confirms it, the consequence extends beyond the group: the finding is published, the group's Groupthink standing is suspended, and the restoration path is defined. This is not punishment — it is the structural consequence of breaking the commitment that defines the framework.

## The Boundaries of Accountability

Accountability is specifically scoped. It covers:

- Responding to harm that has occurred.
- Examining the structural conditions that produced the harm.
- Pursuing repair for the person, group, or resource harmed.
- Publishing findings of floor violations to the network.

It does **not** extend to:

- Preventing harm in progress. *(Defense serves that purpose.)*
- Enforcing compliance with group decisions. *(Reputation, exit, and dispute resolution serve that purpose.)*
- Compelling continued association. *(Voluntary association is unconditional.)*
- Permanent exclusion. *(Every separation has a defined restoration path and expiry.)*
