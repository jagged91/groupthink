# Federation - Implementation
### Groupthink - Version 0.6

---

## What This Is

This document offers starting points for how groups can relate to each other within the Groupthink framework. It describes two forms - federations and compacts - and groups may relate in any way that honours the Federation values and rights.

---

## Federations

A federation is a group of groups that governs shared concerns through iterative deliberation.

### Forming and joining

1. Any two or more groups can form a federation. The founding groups agree on the federation's scope - the specific shared concerns it addresses. This scope is recorded.
2. A new group joins when a current member group vouches for it and the federation discusses. The prospective member should have a genuine stake in the federation's shared concerns.
3. Each member group has one voice, regardless of size.
4. A member group can leave at any time.
5. Scope is bounded. Expanding scope requires consent from all member groups.

### Voices

1. Each member group selects a voice - a person who participates in federation discussions on the group's behalf.
2. A voice carries the group's position. They may only commit to positions the group has agreed to.
3. A voice can be recalled at any time by their group.
4. Everything the voice says and does is recorded.
5. A group is only bound by positions it has consented to.

### The iterative process

Decisions emerge through iterative deliberation - positions flow back and forth between the federation and its member groups until agreement emerges or the matter is set aside.

1. Any member group can raise a concern within the federation's scope.
2. Groups deliberate internally to form positions.
3. Voices convene to present positions, listen, and surface disagreements. The purpose at this stage is understanding, and binding consent comes later.
4. Voices return to their groups. Groups run another internal discussion with the new information.
5. Iterate. Each round should narrow the gap.
6. When agreement seems close, any voice can call for a formal consent round.
7. If consent cannot be reached, the matter is set aside. Groups that agree can act together through a compact.

### Standing decisions in federation

The iterative process takes time by design. Standing decisions allow groups to respond quickly to anticipated scenarios without sacrificing sovereignty.

1. A group makes a standing decision through its normal internal decision process. The standing decision defines: the triggering condition, the position the voice is authorized to take, and any limits.
2. The standing decision is recorded in the group's record and communicated to the federation.
3. When a triggering condition arises, the voice checks whether a standing decision applies. If it does, the voice may act immediately without returning for internal deliberation.
4. If a situation resembles but does not clearly match a standing decision, the voice must return to the group. Ambiguity defaults to deliberation.
5. A group can revoke or modify any standing decision at any time.
6. Standing decisions are especially valuable for: emergency coordination, seasonal resource management, pre-authorized responses to commons invocations, and mutual defense triggers.

### Splitting within federations

1. Splitting is unconditional.
2. When a member group splits, neither fragment automatically keeps federation membership. Fragments apply through the normal process.
3. The federation should welcome fragments in good faith. Refusing a fragment to punish splitting violates the Core.

### Federations of federations

1. Federations can federate. At every layer, the same values apply.
2. Each layer is sovereign. Member governance stands at every layer.
3. Speed matches scope. Higher layers take longer. Deliberation should not be shortened past the point of genuine internal discussion.
4. Scope narrows at each layer. Higher layers handle only concerns that genuinely span all members.
5. Commons-based federations nest following the resource's physical structure — a creek's federation within the river's, within the watershed's. At each layer, scope follows the resource. Higher layers address broader sustainability questions; lower layers address local use and maintenance. *(See Documents/Stewardship/2.Implementation.md § Commons-Based Federations.)*

## Commons-Based Federations

A commons-based federation is a federation that exists because a shared resource exists. Its scope is defined by the resource's physical boundaries, not by group agreement.

1. Any stakeholder group can propose a commons-based federation by identifying the shared resource, its boundaries, and the groups that depend on it.
2. All identified stakeholder groups are invited. Participation is voluntary.
3. The federation's scope is the resource. Decisions outside the resource's scope have no standing.
4. Voice is proportional to stake — how much a group depends on and affects the resource — rather than one-group-one-voice. *(See Documents/Stewardship/2.Implementation.md § Commons-Based Federations for stake declaration and assessment.)*
5. The federation's primary function is to determine what the resource can sustain and to publish that finding to all stakeholders, including non-participating groups.
6. A group that draws from the resource but declines to participate is still bound by what the resource can sustain — because the limits are physical facts, not governance decisions. Any stakeholder may invoke the commons against their use.
7. Commons-based federations may overlap with voluntary federations. A group can participate in both. The commons-based federation addresses the resource; the voluntary federation addresses other shared concerns.

## Compacts

A compact is a voluntary agreement between two or more groups - separate from a federation. It creates no governing body.

1. Any groups can form a compact. They discuss terms, consent, and record it.
2. A compact binds only the groups that consented.
3. Any group can leave a compact at any time.
4. Compacts rely on trust. A broken promise carries reputational consequences.
5. Compacts are visible - recorded in each group's record.
6. Compacts are independent of federation membership.

## Mutual Defense Compacts

A mutual defense compact is an agreement that a threat of irreversible harm to one member is treated as a threat to all. This is the most dangerous form of compact, and it requires extreme safeguards.

### Formation

1. Any two or more groups can form a mutual defense compact. The founding groups agree on: what constitutes a "threat" (defined in advance through standing decisions), how activation works, and what each group commits to provide.
2. The compact is recorded in each group's record with the full terms visible.
3. Any group can leave the compact at any time.

### Activation

1. When a member group faces a threat of irreversible harm beyond its individual capacity, it may invoke the compact.
2. The invocation must identify the specific threat, the evidence, and why it exceeds the invoking group's capacity.
3. An independent confirmation that the threat is real and imminent is required before the compact activates. This confirmation should come from people with no stake in the outcome.
4. If a standing decision applies (the threat matches a pre-defined trigger), participating groups' responses can proceed immediately. If not, each group deliberates through its own process.
5. Each member group retains the right to decline participation by conscience. No group is compelled to act.

### Safeguards

1. No standing defensive force across groups. The compact coordinates a response; it does not maintain a permanent capacity.
2. Activation is specific to the identified threat. When the threat is resolved, the coordinated response ends.
3. Every coordinated action is subject to post-action review by reviewers from outside the compact.
4. The compact expires and must be periodically reauthorized through each member group's normal decision process.
5. The dangers of mutual defense — escalation, entanglement, the creation of a proto-military — should be named openly in the compact's terms.

*(See Documents/Defense/2.Implementation.md § Mutual Defense for the defensive conduct standards that apply to coordinated action.)*

---

## Safeguards

1. Membership is voluntary and continuous.
2. No social coercion for choosing not to join or for leaving.
3. Federations should consider how decisions affect people and communities not represented by any member group.
4. The full federation record is available to all member groups at all times.
