# Core - Implementation
### Groupthink - Version 0.6

---

## What This Is

This document offers starting points for putting the Core values and rights into practice. It is a suggestion. Any group can write its own rules, provided those rules honour the floors and directions described in Documents/Core/0.Values.md.

---

## What Makes a Groupthink Group

A group is a Groupthink group if it:

1. **Adopts the Core.** The group's rules honour every floor, and the group commits to moving in the directions established in Documents/Core/0.Values.md.
2. **Keeps a record.** The group maintains an open, append-only record of its decisions and membership.
3. **Allows exit.** Any member can leave at any time, and their departure is real - respected, unobstructed, and practically possible.

That is all. Everything else is for the group to decide.

---

## The Paper Principle

Everything in this system must be something you can do with people, paper, and a way to talk to each other. Software can help, but it must only do things that could also be done on paper. If a process requires software to function, it is too complex.

This means:
- Any decision process must be explainable to someone who has never used a computer.
- Any record must be something a person can write by hand and another person can read.
- Any communication required by the process must be achievable by talking, writing, or posting a notice.

Software may make these things faster, more accessible, or easier to search. Everything it does must also be achievable without it.

## The Record

Every group keeps a record. The record is a written account of what the group has discussed, what it has decided, and who is a member. The record is:

- **Append-only.** New entries are added to the end. Old entries remain unchanged. If an entry is wrong, a correction is added that references the original.
- **Open to all members.** Any member can read the full record at any time. Any member can keep their own copy. Every copy is equally valid.
- **Open to those affected.** A group's decisions may affect people who are not members. Any person affected by a decision has the right to see the record of that decision and its reasoning. Transparency does not stop at the group's boundary.
- **Written plainly.** The record must be written so that any person can read and understand it.

### The record is a commons

The record is a shared resource built and maintained by the group collectively. It belongs to no individual member and must be preserved for the benefit of all who depend on it - current members, future members, and anyone affected by the group's decisions.

The group is responsible for the stewardship of its record. This means ensuring that the full record remains available, accessible, and intact. When a group stewards shared resources *(see 0.Documents/Stewardship/)*, the same principles apply to the record itself: defined responsibility for its maintenance, accountability to the group, and the obligation that it is never lost, withheld, or allowed to decay through neglect.

## Membership

These are suggested starting points. A group may adopt different rules.

1. When a group is first created, the members are whoever is there at the start. These founding members discuss and consent to the group's first set of rules.
2. A new person joins when a current member vouches for them and the group discusses it. The vouching member attests that the new person is a distinct individual not already a member under another name.
3. A member can leave at any time. It gets written down.
4. A person can be a member of as many groups as they want.
5. Members can use any name they want. Within a group, you use the same name.

## Decisions

Many methods of decision-making honour the Core values. Consent-based governance, majority voting, elected delegates, rotating facilitation, sortition, or methods yet to be invented - all are valid, provided the floors are respected and the directions are maintained.

One starting point is **consent-based governance**:

1. The status quo is the default. Things continue as they are unless someone raises a discussion.
2. Any member can raise a discussion at any time.
3. A discussion must be announced. All members must be notified.
4. Participation is voluntary. Non-participation is treated as standing aside.
5. Consent means "I can live with this." A participant who disagrees yet wishes to allow the group to proceed can stand aside - their disagreement is recorded, and the discussion proceeds.
6. A binding outcome requires the consent of all participants.
7. If consent cannot be reached, the matter is set aside. The status quo continues.

This is one method among many. What matters is that the method chosen honours the Core values: meaningful influence, transparency, free consent, equal standing.

## Standing Decisions

A group can pre-authorize positions on anticipated scenarios — decisions made in advance that take effect when specific conditions arise.

1. A standing decision is made through the group's normal decision process and recorded.
2. A standing decision defines: the condition that triggers it, the action authorized, and any limits on scope.
3. Standing decisions are revocable at any time through the group's normal decision process.
4. When a triggering condition arises, the authorized action may proceed without additional deliberation. In a federation, the group's voice may act on a standing decision without returning for internal discussion.
5. If a situation resembles but does not clearly match a standing decision, the standing decision does not apply. The group deliberates normally.

Standing decisions are especially useful in federations, where iterative deliberation takes time. A group might pre-authorize: "If water quality drops below X, our voice consents to usage restrictions." "If a mutual defense compact is invoked, our voice consents to the defined response." The group retains full sovereignty — it chose the standing decision, and it can revoke it at any time.

## Disputes

1. If people disagree about a rule's meaning, the safest reading is used until the group consents to a new interpretation.
2. When a member alleges a floor has been crossed, the claim cannot be blocked by the party being challenged. The group must examine the claim and record its finding.
3. When a structural claim is contested, either party may request review by people with no stake in the outcome.
4. When an independent review confirms a floor violation, the finding has standing beyond the group. It may be published to the network and may carry network-standing consequences. *(See Documents/Accountability/2.Implementation.md § Network-Standing Consequences.)*

---

## Network Standing

A group is in good standing within the Groupthink framework when it honours every floor, maintains its record, and allows exit. Good standing is not granted by any authority — it is the natural state of any group that keeps its commitments.

When an independent review confirms that a group has crossed a floor:

1. The finding is published to all groups in the violating group's federations and compacts.
2. The group's Groupthink standing is suspended — a structural signal that the group is not currently honouring the commitments that define the framework.
3. Other groups make their own decisions about how to respond. Federations and compacts may choose to suspend the group's participation through their own decision processes.
4. The finding defines what restoration looks like. When the group makes repair and a follow-up independent review confirms it, standing is restored. The restoration is published with the same visibility as the original finding.
5. Standing suspension has a defined duration. At expiry, it must be actively renewed or it lapses.

Suspension is not exile. The group continues to exist. Its members retain all individual rights in every other group. The group can form new relationships with any group willing to associate with it. The path back is always open.

## Changing Rules

A group can change its own rules at any time, through whatever process its current rules define. The only constraint: the new rules must honour the floors.

If a member believes a rule change crosses a floor, they name the violation. The group must address the concern. If the member remains unsatisfied, they are free to leave - and the record shows what happened.

---

## Circles

1. Any member can propose creating a circle - a smaller group inside the group that handles a specific concern.
2. A circle is its own mini-group with its own rules, members, and record, within any constraints the parent group sets.
3. A circle can ask to become fully independent. The parent group must allow a split.

---

## Splitting

1. Any members can leave and form a new group. All who wish to leave must consent among themselves.
2. They take a copy of their own record and rules.
3. Both groups record the split.
4. The parent group must allow a split.

---

## Dormancy

A group can consent to enter dormancy - a period of inactivity. While dormant, decisions are paused. Any single member can wake the group by raising a discussion.
