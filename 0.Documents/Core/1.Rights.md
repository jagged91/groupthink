# Core - Rights
### Groupthink - Version 0.6

---

## What This Is

These are the rights that every person holds in every Groupthink group. They follow from the values in Documents/Core/0.Values.md. They exist because the person exists. They are inherent, and no group can grant or revoke them.

These rights name what is protected. Many different methods can honour them. Where a right implies a process, the value underneath is what binds - the implementation is for groups to determine.

---

## Rights of Every Person

### 1. Voluntary association

A person's relationship to any group is continuous and chosen. A person may withdraw from any aspect of this relationship at any time. No person can be compelled to belong to or remain in any group.

### 2. Free movement

No group may prevent a person from leaving. The right to leave must be real and practical. If leaving would cost a person their shelter, food, or safety, the choice to stay is coerced. *(When a group provides essentials, this right becomes structurally critical - see Documents/Provision/.)*

### 3. Meaningful influence

Every person has the right to meaningfully influence the decisions that affect them. This requires: access, transparency, the ability to dissent, and a genuine path from dissent to change. How this is implemented is for groups to determine.

### 4. No coercion

Every person's consent must be freely given. Pressure, bribery, threats, manipulation, and entrapment - whether toward agreement or silence - are coercion. If you see coercion, name it. That is every person's responsibility.

### 5. Transparency

Every person has the right to understand the decisions that affect them, to see the reasoning behind those decisions, and to access the records that document them.

### 6. Use, not ownership

A person's relationship to material things is use - the act of engaging with something. All things are held in use, and use has different characters:

- **Personal use** - things integrated into a person's body or daily life - is unconditionally protected.
- **Productive use** - a resource a person regularly and actively engages with - is protected, but when contested, duration and consistency of use are relevant.
- **Collective use** - a resource built, maintained, or cultivated together - belongs to the group that created it.
- **Shared use** - a resource available to all - is coordinated by those who share it.

Creative and intellectual works are copyable. Ideas, songs, methods, designs - what someone creates enters the commons of human knowledge. Copying is sharing.

### 7. Dignity

Every person has the right to be treated with basic respect. Harassment, humiliation, and deliberate cruelty are violations regardless of the process used to justify them.

### 8. Conscience

Every person may act according to their deeply held moral convictions. A person who in good conscience cannot participate in a decision may step aside without penalty.

### 9. Repair

When a person is harmed, they have the right to have the harm acknowledged, the structural conditions that produced it examined, and restoration pursued. This right exists whether or not the harming party cooperates, and whether or not the harmed party is a member of any group. *(When a group exercises accountability, see Documents/Accountability/.)*

## Rights of Every Person in a Group

These rights exist at the boundary where individual freedom meets collective life.

### 10. Equal membership

Every member of a group has equal standing. All roles carry equal standing. A person who has been a member for a day has the same rights as one who has been a member for a year.

### 11. The right to know

Every member has the right to see the full record of the group's decisions, discussions, and membership. Transparency within the group is foundational.

### 12. The boundary of consent

A group's decisions bind its members only to the extent those members have consented. Consent must be given actively and freely. A group's internal decisions bind only its own members.

### 13. Departure

When a person leaves, they take what is theirs. Personal use goes with the person unconditionally. Both sides respect the departure. The group continues.

### 14. The right to form groups

Any persons may form a group for any purpose. No permission is required. A group exists when its members say it does.

### 15. The right to split

Any members can leave and form a new group. This right is unconditional. The structure of groups is fractal - the same values apply at every scale.

### 16. Symmetrical association

Voluntary association is symmetrical. Just as a person can leave a group, a group can end its association with a person. This power requires safeguards:

- The default posture is inclusion. Ending association is a last resort.
- A person whose association is ended retains all rights unconditionally - personal use, dignity, free movement, standing in all other groups.
- The process must provide the person full opportunity to be heard and must be subject to independent review.
- Ending association from one group stands alone - each group's membership decisions are independent of every other group's.
- When ending association is a consequence of a confirmed floor violation or harm finding, the Accountability function applies — including structural examination, defined restoration path, and independent review. *(See Documents/Accountability/.)*

### 17. Standing in accountability

When accountability is exercised over a person, they retain: full due process, the right to be heard, access to independent review, a defined path to restoration, and continued access to essentials. No accountability action may strip a person of their Core rights. *(See Documents/Accountability/1.Rights.md for the full set of protections.)*

## Who Is a Person

A person is any being with the capacity for self-awareness and the experience of suffering. Today, this clearly includes every human being. It may include other beings. As understanding grows, the boundary of personhood should grow with it.

Where a being meets this threshold but cannot exercise these rights directly, those who can speak have a greater obligation: to consider their interests, to speak for them, and to err on the side of inclusion when the boundary is uncertain.
