# Document One - The Default Rulebook
### Groupthink - Version 0.3

> This is Document One. It contains the default rules that every Groupthink group inherits. These rules use a discussion-consent model inspired by indigenous governance traditions - Haudenosaunee, Indaba, Ubuntu, and Quaker practice. Groups talk until everyone present can live with the outcome, or set the matter aside. Process only exists when there is friction.

---

## Article One - Membership

*Implements: D0 Article Zero (Voluntary Association)*

1. When a group is first created, the members are whoever is there at the start. These founding members discuss and consent to the group's first set of rules (the rulebook). All founders must consent. If consent cannot be reached, any founder can leave and start their own group.
2. After that, a new person joins in two steps: a current member says "I think this person should join" (vouching), and the group discusses it. If no member objects, the person joins. If anyone objects, the group discusses until consent is reached or the matter is set aside.
3. A member can leave at any time. Just say "I'm leaving." It gets written down, and no one can stop you. *(See D0 Article Zero.)*
4. A member cannot be expelled. If the group has a problem with a member, the group's options are: raise a discussion, form circles, or split. *(See D0 Article Zero.)*
5. If a member stops participating in discussions for 6 consecutive discussion cycles (or a period defined by the rulebook), they are marked "not active." They are still members but are not counted as participants in discussions they don't attend. They can return at any time by participating again.
6. A person can be a member of as many groups as they want at the same time.
7. Members can use any name they want - a real name, a nickname, or a made-up name. Within a group and its circles, you use the same name. In a separate group, you can use a different name. No one can force you to reveal that your name in one group is connected to your name in another.
8. Membership requires the capacity to consent - a person must be able to understand what it means to join, to participate, and to leave. The rulebook defines eligibility conditions. *(See D0 Article Four - Stewardship - for responsibility to those affected by decisions but who cannot be members.)*

## Article Two - Discussions

*Implements: D0 Article One (Equal Power), D0 Article Two (Open Decisions)*

1. **The status quo is the default.** Things continue as they are unless someone raises a discussion.
2. **Any member can raise a discussion at any time.** A discussion is a challenge to something the group is currently doing - or not doing. You don't need permission or supporters.
3. **A discussion must be announced.** All members must be notified that a discussion has been raised, what it concerns, and when it will take place. By default, a discussion cannot produce a binding outcome until at least **2 days** after it is announced. This prevents ambush decisions. The rulebook can change this notice period but it must be at least 1 day.
4. **Participation is voluntary.** Any member can join any discussion. If you don't participate, you are implicitly consenting to whatever the participants decide. If you later disagree with the outcome, you can raise a new discussion (see D1 A2.10).
5. **Discussion has no fixed deadline.** It continues until the participants reach consent or set the matter aside. However, the rulebook can set a maximum discussion period (by default, **30 days**). If no consent is reached by the end of that period, the matter is automatically set aside.
6. **Consent means "I can live with this."** This will not cause harm I'm unwilling to accept, and I will not block the group from moving forward. A participant who disagrees but does not wish to block can **stand aside** - their disagreement is recorded, but the discussion proceeds.
7. **A binding outcome requires the unanimous consent of all participants.** Every person who participated in the discussion must either consent or stand aside. If any participant says "I cannot live with this," the matter is not resolved.
8. **Abstaining.** A member who has seen the discussion but chooses not to participate may formally abstain. Abstaining means "I've seen this and I have no objection." It is recorded as an active choice.
9. **Set-aside.** If consent cannot be reached, the matter is set aside. The status quo continues. The discussion can be raised again later when conditions have changed.
10. **Re-opening a decided matter.** Any member can raise a new discussion about a previous outcome. However, a decided matter cannot be re-opened within one discussion cycle (by default, **14 days**) unless the person raising it can show that something has genuinely changed - new information, new members, changed circumstances. The group decides whether the change is genuine.
11. **Alternatives.** During a discussion, any participant can propose an alternative approach. When alternatives exist, the group discusses all of them. Consent is sought for each alternative in turn, starting with the one that emerged from the discussion most naturally. If the group cannot consent to any alternative, the matter is set aside.

## Article Three - The Process

*Implements: D0 Article Two (Open Decisions)*

1. **Most things never need a discussion.** People act. The group operates. If no one has a problem, no process runs.
2. **When a discussion is raised, a notice period begins.** During this time, members are notified and can choose whether to participate.
3. **After the notice period, the discussion is open.** Participants talk - in person, by letter, by any means available. The goal is understanding. Every participant should speak.
4. **When the group feels ready, any participant can ask: "Can everyone live with this?"** If all participants consent or stand aside, the outcome is binding and recorded. Before consent is sought, any participant can restate or reframe the question - the way a question is asked shapes what is being consented to, and no single participant should control the framing.
5. **If someone cannot consent, the discussion continues.** The group explores why. What would need to change? Can the proposal be modified? Is there an alternative everyone can live with?
6. **If consent still cannot be reached, the matter is set aside.** The status quo continues. The discussion can return later.
7. **Anonymous resistance check (optional).** At any point during a discussion, any participant can request an anonymous resistance check. Each participant writes a number from 0 (no resistance) to 10 (cannot live with this) on a slip of paper. The slips are collected and read aloud. This is a diagnostic tool - it informs the discussion but does not replace consent. The rulebook can make resistance checks mandatory or remove them entirely.

## Article Four - The Record

*Implements: D0 Article Two (Open Decisions), D0 Article Six (The Paper Test)*

1. The record is a list of everything the group has discussed and decided - every discussion raised, every outcome reached, every person who joined or left. New entries are added to the end. Old entries are never changed or deleted.
2. Each entry says: what was discussed, who participated, whether consent was reached or the matter was set aside, and who stood aside (and why, if they chose to say).
3. If there's ever a disagreement about what the group decided, the record is the answer. What's written down is what counts.
4. Any member can keep their own copy of the record. Every copy is equally valid.
5. The record must be written in a way that any person can read and understand.

## Article Five - Circles

*Implements: D0 Article Three (Fractal Structure)*

1. **Any member can propose creating a circle.** A circle is a smaller group inside the group that handles a specific topic. Raise a discussion: "I think we need a circle to handle X."
2. If the group consents, the circle becomes its own mini-group with its own rules, members, and record.
3. The circle must follow any rules the parent group says all circles must follow.
4. Members of the parent group can join the circle if the circle's rules allow it.
5. The circle makes decisions about its own topic using the same discussion-consent process. If something comes up that's outside its topic, it goes to the parent group.
6. The parent group can shut down a circle if the group discusses it and consents to do so.
7. A circle can ask to become fully independent (see D1 Article Six).
8. **Circles are lightweight.** Any two or more members who share an interest can form a circle with minimal overhead. You participate in the circles you care about.
9. A circle is subordinate - created by the parent group, subject to its rules, and can be shut down by it. An atom *(see Document Two)* is an independent group that has joined a larger group as an equal member - it cannot be shut down, cannot be expelled, and follows its own internal rules.

---

## Article Six - Splitting Up

*Implements: D0 Article Zero (Voluntary Association)*

1. Any circle or any group of members can propose becoming their own independent group.
2. The people who want to leave discuss it among themselves. All of them must consent.
3. If they consent, they take a copy of **their own** circle's record and rules. They don't take the parent group's records or other circles' records.
4. The parent group records that they left. The new group records where they came from.
5. The two groups can still work together by raising discussions with each other.
6. **The parent group cannot stop a split.** The right to leave is unconditional. *(See D0 Article Zero.)*
7. **Inherited membership.** If the group that splits is an atom within a larger group *(see Document Two)*, the new group automatically inherits that membership. *(See D2 Article Five.)*

## Article Seven - Resources

*Implements: D0 Article Four (Stewardship), D0 Article Five (No Coercion)*

1. **Property belongs to people.** A member who brings something to the group - money, materials, equipment, a meeting space - still owns it unless they explicitly consent to share it.
2. **Shared resources are held in stewardship.** When a member consents to share a resource with the group, the group discusses and records who is steward of that resource. The steward is responsible for it on behalf of the group.
3. **Stewardship is granted by consent and can be changed by consent.** The group can discuss and reassign stewardship at any time. The current steward must consent to the change unless they are leaving the group.
4. **The record tracks all shared resources and their stewards.** Every resource shared with the group, who shared it, and who is currently steward - all recorded.
5. **When a member leaves, their property goes with them.** Anything a member brought and shared with the group returns to them when they leave, unless they consent to leave it behind.
6. **When a group splits, resources follow their stewards.** Each steward joins whichever new group they choose, and the resources they steward go with them. If both groups need a resource that cannot be divided, the departing and remaining members discuss division before the split is finalized. If no agreement is reached, the resource returns to whoever originally shared it.
7. **Digital records are always copied.** When a group splits, every new group gets a complete copy of the full record.
8. **A group should avoid becoming the only source of things its members need to survive.** When a group controls access to essential resources - income, housing, healthcare, education - the right to leave becomes harder to exercise, and consent becomes harder to give freely. *(See D0 Article Five.)*

## Article Eight - The Rulebook

*Implements: D0 Article Two (Open Decisions), D0 Article One (Equal Power)*

1. Every group has a rulebook. If a group doesn't write one, the defaults in this document are used instead.
2. The rulebook spells out: how people join, how long notice periods are, how long discussions can last before automatic set-aside, what topics circles handle, whether resistance checks are used, and how to change the rulebook itself.
3. The rulebook is part of the record - it exists because the group consented to create it.
4. **Changing the rulebook requires a discussion with heightened participation.** By default, all active members must be notified and given an extended notice period of **5 days** (instead of the default 2). The rulebook can adjust this, but the notice period for rulebook changes must always be at least double the normal notice period.
5. The rulebook can never break D0 Article Zero or any other article in Document Zero.
6. If a group is a circle inside a bigger group, its rulebook can't break the bigger group's main rules.

## Article Nine - When People Disagree

*Implements: D0 Article Two (Open Decisions)*

1. If two outcomes say opposite things, the newer one governs - unless the older one was a rulebook change. Rulebook changes take precedence over regular decisions.
2. If a circle's decision breaks one of the parent group's main rules, the parent's rule wins.
3. If people disagree about what a rule means, someone raises a discussion. Until the group reaches consent on the new wording, the safest reading of the rule is used - the one that keeps things closest to how they already are.
4. If two copies of the record got out of sync, both versions are kept as history. Any decision that only appears in one version doesn't count until it's discussed and consented to by the full group.
5. **When members disagree about how any rule applies to a specific situation, any member can raise it as a discussion.** The group is its own judge. If a group gets big enough that this becomes impractical, it can create a circle to handle disputes.

---

## Article Ten - Dormancy

*Implements: D0 Article Zero (Voluntary Association)*

1. A group can consent to enter dormancy - a deliberate pause where no discussions are expected and no inactivity clocks run.
2. While dormant, no binding decisions can be made. Any member can raise a discussion to end dormancy.
3. Dormancy is recorded. Members remain members. The group can wake up at any time.
