# Document Three - Federations
### Groupthink - Version 0.4

> This is Document Three. It establishes how groups coordinate with each other. A **federation** is a group of groups that governs shared concerns through back-and-forth discussion. Member groups are equal partners.
>
> Federations follow the commons (Document Zero), respect the rights of persons (Document One), and use the governance tools defined in Document Two.

---

## The Principle - Iterative Consent

A single group governs itself through discussion and consent (Document Two). When multiple groups share a concern, they need a way to discuss it together. A federation is that shared discussion.

A federation governs only the **shared concerns** that its members have agreed to address together. Each member group stays in charge of its own internal affairs. *(Scope boundary: the federation addresses shared concerns defined in its rulebook. Everything else belongs to the member groups.)*

Federation decisions are also limited by the commons (Document Zero).

---

## Article One - Forming and Joining a Federation

*Implements: D1 A3 (Fractal Structure), D1 A0 (Voluntary Association)*

1. **Any two or more groups can form a federation.** The founding groups discuss and agree on the federation's scope — what shared concerns it will address. This scope is recorded in the federation's rulebook.
2. **A new group joins through the standard membership process.** A current member group vouches for the new group, the federation discusses, and if no member group objects, the new group joins. *(See D2 A1.2.)* The membership discussion should establish that the prospective member has a genuine stake in the federation's shared concerns.
3. **Each member group has one voice,** regardless of how many people it contains. This is a deliberate design choice: at the federation level, the unit of participation is the group, and groups are equal. The alternative — population-weighted representation — would allow large groups to dominate small ones, making federation membership coercive. *(See D1 A1.)* The federation rulebook can, with the consent of all members, adopt alternative voice structures if the member groups agree.
4. **A member group can leave at any time.** *(See D1 A0.)* Leaving means the group is no longer bound by federation decisions and no longer takes part in federation discussions. A group that leaves may rejoin through the normal membership process.
5. **A member group cannot be removed.** *(See D1 A0, D2 A1.4.)*
6. **Scope is bounded.** The federation only addresses the shared concerns defined in its rulebook. A member group can propose expanding the scope, but this requires consent from all member groups (it is a rulebook change). Decisions outside the federation's scope have no standing. *(Scope boundary: a federation may coordinate the use of a commons resource within its limits, but the limits themselves belong to the commons (Document Zero).)*

---

## Article Two - Voices

*Implements: D1 A1 (Equal Power), D1 A2 (Open Decisions)*

1. **Each member group selects a voice** — a person who takes part in federation discussions on the group's behalf. The voice is selected through the group's own D2 consent process.
2. **A voice carries the group's position.** They present it, explain it, and listen to other groups' positions. During rounds of discussion, a voice may talk, ask questions, and explore options — but may only commit to positions the group has agreed to.
3. **A voice can be recalled at any time.** The group discusses and agrees to replace their voice.
4. **A voice's actions are recorded.** Everything the voice says and does in federation discussions is part of the federation record, visible to all member groups.
5. **A group is only bound by positions it has consented to.** If a voice commits beyond what the group agreed to, the group can disavow the commitment and send a correction.
6. **In software, voices may be unnecessary.** When a group can publish its agreed position directly, the voice role exists only for in-person meetings or live discussion.

## Article Three - The Iterative Process

*Implements: D1 A1 (Equal Power), D1 A2 (Open Decisions)*

This is the heart of federation governance. Decisions emerge through **iterative deliberation** — positions flow back and forth between the federation and its member groups until agreement emerges or the matter is set aside.

1. **Any member group can raise a concern.** The concern is announced to all member groups. It must fall within the federation's scope (A1.6).
2. **Groups deliberate internally.** Each member group runs its own D2 discussion to form a position on the concern. Groups that choose not to take part are implicitly consenting. *(See D2 A2.4.)*
3. **Voices convene.** The voices bring their groups' positions to the federation discussion. They present positions, listen, surface disagreements, and explore options. **No binding consent is sought in this round.** The purpose is understanding.
4. **Voices return to their groups.** Each voice reports back: what other groups said, what concerns were raised, what options were proposed, where agreement and disagreement lie. Each group runs another internal D2 discussion with the new information. Positions may shift, hold, or new options may emerge.
5. **Iterate.** Steps 3 and 4 repeat as many times as needed. Each round should narrow the gap. The federation's rulebook may set a maximum number of rounds or a maximum time period, after which the matter is automatically set aside.
6. **Consent round.** When the voices believe agreement is close, any voice can call for a formal consent round. Each voice presents their group's final position: consent, stand aside, or block. Blocks must be seconded by another group's voice. *(See D2 A2.8.)* If consent is reached, the outcome is recorded and binding on all member groups that consented or stood aside.
7. **Set-aside.** If consent cannot be reached, the matter is set aside. *(See D2 A2.10.)* The status quo continues. Groups that agree with each other can choose to act together through a compact (A5) without binding the federation.
8. **Supermajority escalation at federation level.** When a matter has been set aside **3 times** on the same underlying concern (the federation rulebook can adjust this threshold but not below 2), any member group can invoke a supermajority round. *(See D2 A2.13.)* In a federation supermajority round: **(a)** all member groups are notified with a double notice period and the full history of prior attempts; **(b)** each member group runs an internal deliberation; **(c)** the decision passes if at least **80%** of participating member groups consent (the federation rulebook can adjust this threshold but not below 75%); **(d)** dissenting member groups receive **enhanced exit rights** — they may leave the federation with all compact relationships and other federation memberships intact, and their departure cannot be treated as grounds for punitive action by any remaining member; **(e)** the outcome is recorded as a supermajority decision and flagged as such. A supermajority round cannot be used for federation rulebook changes, which always require full consent.
9. **Stand-aside at federation level.** A group that stands aside on a federation decision is bound only by decisions it consented to. The stand-aside is recorded. The decision applies only to the groups that consented. If a member group stands aside on more than half the decisions in a period defined by the federation's rulebook (by default, **3 consecutive decision cycles**), any member group can raise a **scope review** — a discussion about whether the standing-aside group's concerns still genuinely overlap with the federation's scope. A scope review is not removal *(see D1 A0.5)* — it is a good-faith conversation. The group under review participates fully and may re-engage, narrow its involvement, or choose to leave.
10. **Constitutional check.** At any point during deliberation, any member group can raise a concern that a proposed decision violates Document One (rights of persons). If raised, the federation must address the concern before proceeding to a consent round. The federation must engage with the constitutional question.
11. **The commons clause.** At any point during deliberation, any member group can invoke the commons: *"This decision exceeds what the commons can sustain."* The invocation must cite specific, observable evidence. *(See D0 A4, D2 A2.14.)* When invoked, the federation must examine the evidence before proceeding to a consent round. The invocation and the federation's response are recorded. Re-raising the same concern requires new evidence or changed conditions. An invocation without specific evidence is treated as a normal objection within the iterative process.
12. **Commons escalation.** A federation may receive an escalated commons invocation from an external stakeholder — a person or group that depends on the same resource but is not a federation member. *(See D0 A4.5.)* The federation must examine the evidence as it would for an internal invocation. External stakeholders have standing on commons questions because the commons belongs to everyone who depends on it, not only to federation members.

## Article Four - Splitting and Federation Membership

*Implements: D1 A0 (Voluntary Association), D1 A4 (No Coercion)*

1. **Splitting is unconditional.** Any group can split at any time for any reason. *(See D1 A0, D2 A6.)* The federation cannot prevent, delay, or punish a split.
2. **When a member group splits, neither fragment automatically keeps federation membership** — except where enhanced exit rights *(D2 A6.8)* or integrity-related split protections *(D2 A6.9)* apply. In those cases, the protected fragment retains membership by default; the other fragment applies through the normal process.
3. **The federation should welcome fragments in good faith.** The iterative process (A3) applies to membership discussions. Fragments that have a genuine connection to the federation's shared concerns should ordinarily be admitted. Refusing a fragment to punish the act of splitting violates D1 A0.
4. **During the membership discussion, fragments may take part as observers.** They can present their perspective and answer questions, but they do not have a voice (cannot consent, block, or stand aside) until admitted.
5. **The cost of splitting is bounded.** A fragment that is not admitted to one federation keeps all its other relationships — other federation memberships, compacts, and internal self-governance. *(See D1 A4.)*
6. **New groups that did not come from a split follow normal membership rules.** *(See A1.2.)*

## Article Five - Compacts

*Implements: D1 A0 (Voluntary Association), D1 A3 (Fractal Structure)*

A compact is a voluntary agreement between two or more groups. *(Scope boundary: a compact is separate from a federation — it creates no governing body and no shared decision-making structure. Federations are defined above; compacts are defined here.)* A compact is a mutual promise, recorded in each group's record.

1. **Any groups can form a compact.** The groups discuss the terms, consent, and record the compact. A compact can cover anything: mutual defence, resource sharing, coordination, information exchange.
2. **A compact binds only the groups that consented to it.**
3. **Any group can leave a compact at any time.** *(See D1 A0.)* The departure is recorded. The compact continues among the remaining groups unless its terms say otherwise.
4. **Compacts cannot compel.** A compact is a promise. If a group does not honour a compact, the other groups may dissolve the compact, raise a discussion, or form new arrangements. No group can be punished for failing to act on a compact. *(See D1 A4.)*
5. **Compacts are visible.** Both the terms of the compact and any actions taken under it are recorded in each group's record. *(See D1 A2.)* A group's compact history — including broken compacts — is part of the record and may be considered by other groups in future membership, federation, and compact decisions. The consequence is reputational, not punitive.
6. **Compacts can exist between federation members, between federations, or between any groups.** They are independent of federation membership.
7. **Compacts cannot override commons constraints.** A compact that allows exceeding what the commons can sustain is void for that provision. *(See D0 A2.)*

## Article Six - The Fractal

*Implements: D1 A3 (Fractal Structure)*

1. **Federations can form federations.** A federation of groups can itself become a member of a larger federation. At every layer, the rules are the same — this document, applied to members that happen to be federations. The iterative process (A3) applies at every layer.
2. **Each layer is sovereign.** A larger federation's decisions do not override a member federation's internal governance or the internal governance of any member group. If a member federation's internal consent changes after the larger federation has decided something, it can raise a re-opening discussion or leave.
3. **Each layer is self-sufficient.** If a federation dissolves, its member groups continue to exist on their own. They can reform, join other federations, coordinate through compacts, or go their separate ways.
4. **Speed matches scope.** Discussions at higher layers take longer because each member must run its own internal process. Software can speed up communication, but the discussion process should not be shortened past the point where genuine internal discussion can happen.
5. **Scope narrows at each layer.** A federation of federations should have a narrower scope than its member federations — handling only concerns that genuinely span all members. Higher layers claiming authority over matters that could be handled locally should be resisted.

---

## Article Seven - Safeguards

*Implements: D1 A4 (No Coercion), D0 A3 (Responsibility)*

1. **Federation membership is voluntary.** A group's decision to join a federation must be internally consented. A group's decision to stay must be continuously voluntary. If staying creates duties the group's own members did not consent to, the group can leave. *(See D1 A0.)*
2. **No social coercion.** A group that chooses not to join a federation, or that leaves one, must not face social consequences for that choice. Pressuring a group to join violates D1 A4.
3. **Responsibility.** Federations should consider how their decisions affect people, communities, and living things not represented by any member group. *(See D0 A3.)*
4. **Essential resources.** A federation that coordinates access to resources its members need to survive should be especially careful. The right to leave, the right to block, and the right to stand aside must remain livable. If a federation's scope touches essential resources, it should actively work to ensure that alternatives exist. *(See D1 A4.)* *(Scope boundary: governance of the resources themselves belongs to the commons (Document Zero). The federation coordinates use within commons constraints.)*
5. **Record transparency.** The federation's full record — every discussion, every round of deliberation, every position taken by every voice, every consent and every block — is available to all member groups at all times. *(See D1 A2.)*
